# 📣 Communication & Stakeholder Management

> *"The product manager must ensure that everyone on the team — and the key stakeholders — deeply understand the product vision, the product strategy, and the product priorities."*
> — Marty Cagan

**Time allocation: ~15% (~6h/week)**

Communication is the glue that holds the product operating model together. A PM who builds the right thing but fails to communicate it loses stakeholder trust, creates misalignment, and eventually loses the ability to protect the team's focus.

---

## Why It Matters (Cagan)

Cagan argues that great PMs are great evangelists. They don't just discover and deliver — they make sure everyone in the organisation understands *why* decisions were made, *what* the team is learning, and *where* the product is heading.

Without strong communication, three things go wrong:
1. Stakeholders fill the vacuum with their own assumptions — and start making product decisions themselves
2. Engineers lose context — and can't make good autonomous decisions
3. Leadership loses confidence — and starts micromanaging the roadmap

---

## The Three Communication Audiences

### 1. Your Squad (Engineers, Designers)
**Goal:** Context, not just tasks. The team should understand the *why* behind everything.

What they need from you:
- The customer problem, not just the feature spec
- The outcome you're optimising for
- The constraints and trade-offs you've already considered
- Fast answers when they are blocked

**Formats:** Sprint planning, daily standups, async Slack updates, 1:1s with key team members.

### 2. Stakeholders (Internal: Sales, Legal, CS, Ops)
**Goal:** Transparency and trust. Stakeholders don't need to approve your roadmap — but they need to know what's happening and feel heard.

What they need from you:
- Regular release updates — what shipped and what it means for them
- A clear channel to submit requests (and a clear explanation of how requests are prioritised)
- Early visibility into things that affect their work before they are surprised by them
- Honest status on things they care about

**Cagan's rule:** Stakeholders are not customers. Their requests are inputs to discovery, not requirements. Treat their feedback seriously, but you own the prioritisation.

**Formats:** Post-release updates (Slack or email), bi-weekly stakeholder review, ad-hoc briefings for major initiatives.

### 3. Leadership (CEO, Management)
**Goal:** Alignment and confidence. Leadership should never feel like they need to intervene in the roadmap to protect business outcomes — because they trust that you already are.

What they need from you:
- Quarterly OKR alignment and mid-quarter progress
- Strategic trade-offs explained clearly — what you chose and what you didn't
- Early warning on risks — don't let them find out from someone else
- Results of major bets — what worked, what didn't, what you learned

**Formats:** Quarterly planning sessions, bi-weekly OKR check-ins, escalations when needed.

## The Weekly Product Update

A short, consistent weekly update is one of the highest-leverage communication habits a PM can build. It creates a paper trail, builds trust, and prevents surprise.

**Structure (keep it to 5 minutes to read):**

```
## Week of [date]

**What we shipped:**
- [Item] — [what it means / why it matters]

**What we learned (from discovery):**
- [Interview insight / experiment result]

**What we're working on next:**
- [Top 1-2 priorities]

**Risks / things to flag:**
- [Anything stakeholders or leadership should know]
```

Send to: squad + relevant stakeholders. Post in the product Slack channel.

---

## The Product Weekly (Internal PM Meeting)

In the team product weekly:
- Present what you worked on (1 min max — be crisp)
- Share key updates relevant to the wider team
- Share what you learned — from interviews, experiments, data
- Ask for input on trade-offs or decisions you're wrestling with

---

## Handling Stakeholder Requests

**When a stakeholder brings you a request:**
1. Thank them and acknowledge the request genuinely
2. Ask clarifying questions: who is affected? how often? what's the workaround?
3. Add it to the backlog as an opportunity, not a requirement
4. Close the loop: "I've logged this. Here's roughly where it sits relative to current priorities. I'll come back to you when we get closer."
5. Never promise dates for unplanned requests

**The PM as shock absorber:** Your job is to ensure stakeholder requests go through discovery before they hit the engineering team. Protect the team's focus. Do not let every request become a ticket.

---

## Cagan's Communication Principles

- **No surprises** — stakeholders should never hear major news about the product from someone other than you
- **Radical transparency on priorities** — if something is not on the roadmap, say so clearly and explain why
- **Evangelize the vision constantly** — repeat it until you're tired of saying it. The team and stakeholders need to hear it many more times than you think.
- **Acknowledge when you're wrong** — when an experiment fails or a bet doesn't pay off, own it and share what you learned. Trust is built through honesty, not spin.

---

## Communication Checklist (Weekly)

- [ ] Weekly product update sent to squad and stakeholders
- [ ] Release notes communicated after any shipping
- [ ] Responded to all stakeholder requests (even with "logged, will revisit")
- [ ] Squad has context for current sprint priorities
- [ ] Leadership aware of any risks or blockers
