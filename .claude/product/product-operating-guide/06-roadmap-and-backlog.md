# 🗺️ Roadmap & Backlog

> *"A roadmap is not a commitment to build a set of features. It is a plan to achieve a set of outcomes."*
> — Marty Cagan

---

## Why This Distinction Matters

Most roadmaps are lists of features with dates. Cagan argues this is a fundamental mistake. A feature-based roadmap:

- Commits to solutions before problems are understood
- Removes the team's ability to discover better solutions
- Sets expectations with stakeholders that are almost always wrong
- Measures success in outputs, not outcomes

An outcome-based roadmap says: *"This quarter, we are focused on reducing time-to-first-value for new lawyers. Here are the initiatives we believe will move that needle."* The initiatives may change as you learn — the outcome target stays stable.

---

## Roadmap Principles (Cagan)

1. **Epics should be defined as outcomes, not outputs**
   Bad: *"Build notification system"*
   Good: *"Improve lawyer response rate to 70%+ within 24h"*

2. **Plan maximum 1 quarter ahead in detail**
   Startups operate in fast-changing environments. If you are planning the next 12 months in ticket-level detail, you are not agile — and it will not happen that way anyway.

3. **Use themes for anything beyond 1 quarter**
   Beyond the current quarter, use directional themes: *"H2: Focus on platform stability and enterprise readiness."* No features, no dates.

4. **The roadmap is a hypothesis, not a contract**
   Stakeholders need to understand this. The roadmap represents your best current thinking. New information from discovery will change it — and that's good, not a failure.

5. **Say no — in writing**
   Explicitly list what is NOT on the roadmap this quarter, and why. This is as important as what is on it.

---

## Roadmap Structure: Now / Next / Later

| Horizon | Timeframe | Detail Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Now** | Current sprint / this month | Full tickets, acceptance criteria, owners |
| **Next** | Next 1-2 months | Epics with outcome framing, roughly sized |
| **Later** | Beyond that | Themes only, no commitments |

---

## Backlog Management

A healthy backlog is not a long list — it is a **prioritised, outcome-linked** set of the next things to build.

**Rules for backlog health:**
- Every item should be linked to an Epic (which is linked to a product outcome)
- Items without a clear outcome link get parked or removed
- Priority should reflect the current quarter's OKRs — not historical sentiment
- Refine the backlog weekly, not just at sprint planning
- Engineers should always know what to pick up next without asking

**Prioritisation mental models:**

- *Why do we need to do this now?* (urgency)
- *How many users are affected?* (reach)
- *Is there a workaround?* (severity)
- *What is the underlying assumption — have we validated it?* (risk)
- *Does this move our current OKR outcome?* (alignment)

---

## Epic Structure

Each Epic should contain:

1. **Outcome statement** — what metric or customer behaviour are we trying to change?
2. **Baseline** — where is that metric today?
3. **Target** — where do we want it to be by when?
4. **Key assumptions** — what do we believe that, if wrong, would kill this initiative?
5. **Discovery status** — what have we validated? What are we still testing?
6. **Linked tickets** — the user stories that make up this Epic

---

## Backlog Refinement Checklist (Weekly)

- [ ] All tickets for the next sprint have clear problem statements and acceptance criteria
- [ ] Priority order reflects current OKRs — not inertia
- [ ] Any tickets without an outcome link have been addressed or removed
- [ ] Upcoming epics have a clear outcome framing
- [ ] "Later" items have been reviewed — any that should move up or be removed?
