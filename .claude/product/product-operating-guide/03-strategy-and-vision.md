# ♠️ Strategy & Vision

> *"The product vision is the north star. The product strategy is how we plan to get there. Without both, we are just reacting."*
> — Marty Cagan

**Time allocation: ~15% (~6h/week)**

Strategy is the filter that prevents reactive feature-building. It is how you say no with confidence. Without a clear strategy, every request feels equally urgent, and the roadmap becomes a wish list.

---

## Why It Matters (Cagan)

Cagan argues that most product teams lack a real strategy — they have a roadmap, which is not the same thing. A roadmap is a list of things to build. A strategy explains *why* those things and not others, *in what order*, and *toward what end*.

The best PMs use strategy as a decision-making tool. When a new request comes in, a strong strategy makes it easy to evaluate: does this move us toward our vision, or away from it?

---

## The Three Layers

### 1. Product Vision (2–5 years)
The inspiring future you are working toward. Not a feature, not a metric — a picture of the world as it should be for your customers.

**Cagan's rules for a great vision:**
- It should be **customer-centric** — about their world, not your product
- It should be **inspiring** — something the team genuinely believes in and is motivated by
- It should be **ambitious but plausible** — a stretch, not a fantasy
- It should be **stable** — vision should not change with every quarter
- It should be **aligned with the company mission**

*Example frame: "A world where every lawyer in Germany can manage their caseload without administrative overhead getting in the way of doing actual legal work."*

### 2. Product Strategy (quarterly / annual)
How you plan to make progress toward the vision — in this quarter, with these constraints, for these customers.

**Cagan's rules for a strong strategy:**
- It must make **explicit trade-offs** — what you will NOT pursue is as important as what you will
- It should identify the **key obstacles** between you and the vision
- It should specify **which customer segments** you are optimising for right now
- It must be **informed by discovery** — not invented in a conference room

Key strategic questions to answer each quarter:
- What is the single most important thing we can move this quarter?
- Which customer segment are we focusing on and why?
- What are we explicitly deprioritising — and why?
- What is the biggest assumption underlying our strategy, and how will we test it?
- What would have to be true for this strategy to fail?

### 3. Product Principles
The standing rules that guide day-to-day decisions without needing to escalate every trade-off.

Examples of good product principles:
- *"Lawyer time is scarce — every interaction should be faster than the alternative."*
- *"We do not build features only one customer asked for."*
- *"Speed of learning beats perfection of solution."*
- *"We are not a CRM. We do not try to replace the lawyer's existing tools."*

Good principles make it possible for engineers and designers to make decisions aligned with strategy, without checking with the PM every time.

---

## Strategic Duties (Quarterly)

- **Determine quarterly direction** — what is the biggest bet we are making this quarter?
- **Make explicit trade-offs** — document what we are not doing and why
- **Identify major risks** — what could make our strategy wrong? How do we test it?
- **Align leadership** — ensure management and stakeholders understand and agree with the direction
- **Cascade context** — make sure the squad understands the why behind every initiative

---

## Strategy Anti-Patterns (Cagan)

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | The Problem |
|---|---|---|
| Roadmap as strategy | "Our strategy is to ship these 12 features" | No direction, no trade-offs, no learning |
| HiPPO-driven strategy | CEO or biggest customer dictates the roadmap | Ignores the broader market, kills discovery |
| Feature parity strategy | "We need to match competitor X" | Customer needs trump competitor features |
| Boiling the ocean | Everything is a priority | Nothing is a priority |
| Annual lock-in | Strategy set in January, never revisited | Startups change too fast for annual plans |

---

## Strategic Review Checklist (Quarterly)

- [ ] Is our vision still relevant — or do we need to update it?
- [ ] What is the key strategic bet for this quarter?
- [ ] What are we explicitly NOT doing, and is that documented?
- [ ] Are our OKRs derived from strategy, or are they disconnected?
- [ ] Does the whole squad understand the strategy — not just the PM?
- [ ] What is the riskiest assumption in our strategy, and how will we test it?
