# 🤖 AI & Toolbox

A collection of tools, mental models, and principles for making good decisions and working effectively as a PM.

---

## AI: Where It Helps Most

AI is a force multiplier for PMs — it doesn't replace judgment, but it dramatically reduces the cost of certain tasks, freeing up time for the high-value work that only a human can do.

| Task | How AI helps |
|---|---|
| **User story writing** | Draft ticket descriptions and acceptance criteria from a problem statement |
| **Feedback synthesis** | Summarise patterns across interview notes, tickets, and reviews |
| **Experiment design** | Suggest experiment formats for a given assumption |
| **Competitive research** | Summarise competitor positioning, features, pricing |
| **Release notes** | Draft release notes from a list of shipped Jira items |
| **OKR drafting** | Turn a vague goal into structured Objectives and Key Results |
| **Roadmap framing** | Translate a list of initiatives into outcome-framed roadmap items |
| **Strategy stress-testing** | Ask AI to argue against your strategy — surface blind spots |
| **Prototyping / MVP concepts** | Generate wireframe descriptions, user flow suggestions, rough concepts |

**What AI should not replace:**
- Talking to real users — no substitute
- Making prioritisation trade-offs — requires business context and judgment
- Setting strategy — requires understanding of the full competitive, customer, and business landscape
- Building trust with stakeholders — a human relationship

## Mental Models for Prioritisation

**The fundamental questions before any item goes into the sprint:**
1. *Why do we need to do this now?* — Is there genuine urgency or just noise?
2. *How many users are affected?* — Reach matters. One customer's problem ≠ a product priority.
3. *Is there a workaround?* — If yes, how painful is it? How long can we live with it?
4. *What is the assumption behind this item?* — Have we validated it, or are we guessing?
5. *Does this move our current OKR outcome?* — If not, it goes to the backlog.

**RICE scoring (when you need to be more rigorous):**
- **Reach** — how many users in a given period?
- **Impact** — how much does it move the needle per user? (3 = massive, 1 = low)
- **Confidence** — how sure are we about reach and impact? (as a %)
- **Effort** — how many person-weeks?
- Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort

## OKR Principles

1. **Set the Objective first** — What is the inspiring, directional goal for this quarter?
2. **Baseline before you target** — You cannot set a good target without knowing where you currently are
3. **2-4 Key Results per Objective** — measurable, specific, time-bound
4. **70% confidence rule** — if you're 100% sure you'll hit it, it's not ambitious enough
5. **Check in every 2 weeks** — don't wait for quarter-end to discover you're off track
6. **Kill what's not moving** — don't sunk-cost your way to the end of a quarter on a bet that isn't working

## Roadmap Mental Models

- Epics should be outcomes, not features
- Plan 1 quarter ahead in detail — beyond that, use themes
- The roadmap is a hypothesis, not a contract
- What you say no to is as important as what you say yes to

## Discovery Mental Models (Cagan)

- *"The riskiest thing is to build something no one wants."* — Test before you build.
- *"Prototypes are 10x cheaper than products."* — Validate with the cheapest possible artifact.
- *"You are not the user."* — Your intuitions are a starting point, not a conclusion.
- *"Strong opinions, loosely held."* — Form views, but update them rapidly when evidence says otherwise.
- *"Opportunity before solution."* — Fully understand the problem before proposing anything.

---

## Delivery Mental Models

- *"The goal is outcomes, not velocity."* — Shipping fast in the wrong direction is not progress.
- *"Context > tasks."* — Engineers with full context make better decisions than engineers with detailed specs.
- *"Protect the team's focus."* — Every interruption has a compounding cost.
- *"Done means measurably working."* — A feature is not done when it ships. It is done when it achieves its intended outcome.
