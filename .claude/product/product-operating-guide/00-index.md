# 🎯 Product Operating Guide

> *"It doesn't matter how good your engineering team is if they are not given something worthwhile to build."*
> — Marty Cagan, Inspired

This is the operating system for how I work as a PM. It is built on Marty Cagan's **Product Operating Model** — empowered teams given real problems to solve, not features to build. Discovery and delivery run in parallel, outcomes beat outputs, and the best PMs are missionaries, not mercenaries.

## Cagan's Core Principles

**1. Outcome over Output**
The goal is never to ship features. The goal is to solve customer problems in ways that work for the business. Shipping is not success — measurable impact is. A team can ship 100 features and still fail.

**2. Empowered Teams, Not Feature Factories**
Great PMs give their teams problems to solve, not solutions to implement. An empowered team has the context, autonomy, and accountability to discover and deliver the best solution. The PM's job is to ensure the team knows *why*, not just *what*.

**3. Continuous Discovery**
Discovery is not a phase — it is a weekly habit. You should always be talking to customers, running experiments, and reducing risk in parallel with delivery, not before it. Teresa Torres: *"Continuous discovery is a cadence, not a methodology."*

**4. The Four Risks**
Every product initiative carries four risks that must be addressed before committing to build:

- **Value risk** — Will customers actually want this?
- **Usability risk** — Can they figure out how to use it?
- **Feasibility risk** — Can we build it with what we have?
- **Viability risk** — Does it work for our business model, legal, and ethical constraints?

**5. Fall in Love with the Problem, Not the Solution**
Your job is to deeply understand the problem space. Solutions are hypotheses. Only evidence from real users validates them. The best PMs resist the urge to jump to solutions — they sit with the problem until it is truly understood.

**6. The Four Knowledge Areas of Great PMs**
Cagan identifies four areas that separate great PMs from average ones:

- Deep knowledge of **the customer** — their pains, jobs-to-be-done, context, and constraints
- Deep knowledge of **the data** — what the numbers actually say, not what we hope they say
- Deep knowledge of **the business** — constraints, revenue model, legal, how we make money
- Deep knowledge of **the market and domain** — competitors, trends, and the space we operate in

**7. Missionaries, Not Mercenaries**
The best product teams are missionaries — they believe deeply in the mission and are motivated by impact. Mercenaries execute a roadmap. Missionaries solve a problem. Your job as PM is to build and sustain that belief in the team.

## TLDR — What Good PMs Do Daily

1. **Discover** continuously — talk to users, run experiments, reduce uncertainty
2. **Define outcomes** — set clear goals and measure whether you moved them
3. **Set strategy** — choose direction and decide what *not* to build
4. **Enable delivery** — give engineers clarity, autonomy, and fast feedback
5. **Communicate** — align your team, stakeholders, and leadership on the why
