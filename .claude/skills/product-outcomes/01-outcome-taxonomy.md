# What Is an Outcome?

The foundational definition comes from Josh Seiden (*Outcomes Over Output*):

> **"An outcome is a change in human behaviour that drives business results."**

This is the north star. Notice what it contains: a *change* (not a state), in *human behaviour* (observable, measurable), that *drives business results* (connected to value). If any of these three elements is missing, you're probably not looking at a real outcome.

Teresa Torres sharpens this for product teams: a product outcome measures **a customer behaviour in the product** or **a customer sentiment about the product** — and it functions as a **leading indicator** of lagging business results.

---

## Output vs. Outcome

An output is something you build or produce. An outcome is the impact that output has.

| Output | Outcome |
|---|---|
| We built integrations with Slack, Gmail, and Zapier | Customers are actively using integrations to connect their workflows (reducing manual steps) |
| We shipped a redesigned onboarding flow | % of new users completing onboarding rose from 34% to 58% |
| We launched push notifications | Users who receive a nudge return within 24 hours at 2× the rate of users who don't |
| We built an admin dashboard | Support tickets about account management dropped 40% |

Integrations sitting unused are pure output — no value created. Customers *using* them to solve problems is the outcome. This distinction matters because teams that ship outputs feel productive without necessarily creating value.

## The Three-Layer Hierarchy

Teresa Torres identifies three types of outcomes, and they are not interchangeable:

### 1. Business Outcomes
Measure the health of the business. Typically financial or strategic metrics derived from the revenue model.

Examples: grow revenue, increase market share, reduce churn, increase average contract value.

**The problem with using these as team targets**: They are lagging indicators — far downstream from what any one team does day to day. Many factors outside the team's control influence them. A team told to "grow revenue" has no clear path to know whether their work is moving the needle.

### 2. Product Outcomes ← The Sweet Spot
Measure a customer behaviour in the product, or customer sentiment about the product. These are **leading indicators** of business outcomes — they move faster and they're closer to the team's work.

Examples: 7-day retention rate, % of users who complete a core workflow, frequency of repeat sessions, task completion rate, time-to-value for new users.

**Why this is the right level**: Product teams have real agency here. They can run experiments, observe behavioural shifts, and connect their work to the metric. It's specific enough to act on, broad enough to allow many possible solutions.

### 3. Traction Metrics
Measure adoption of a specific feature. These feel like outcomes but are too narrow.

Examples: # of searches conducted, # of notifications sent, # of times a specific button was clicked.

**The problem**: Traction metrics tempt teams to optimise for usage of *their solution* rather than *customer value*. More searches often means more confusion — customers are struggling to find what they need. Traction metrics can go up while the customer experience gets worse.

---

## The Goldilocks Test

When evaluating whether you have the right type of outcome, ask:

- **Too broad (business outcome)?** → Can the team directly influence this, or are there too many variables outside their control?
- **Too narrow (traction metric)?** → Does hitting this number guarantee the customer is getting real value, or could it go up for the wrong reasons?
- **Just right (product outcome)?** → Is this a observable change in customer behaviour that we have evidence (or strong reason to believe) connects to business health?

The sweet spot gives the team **latitude to explore the opportunity space** (multiple ways to move the metric) while keeping them **anchored to something that matters** (connected to business value via a plausible causal chain).

---

## The Leading Indicator Principle

Product outcomes work because they are leading indicators of lagging business outcomes. Engagement today predicts retention next quarter. Task completion rate today predicts NPS next year.

This is why teams should care about getting product outcomes right: if you pick a leading indicator that doesn't actually predict the business outcome you care about, you can optimise hard for it and still not move the business. Choosing the right product outcome requires thinking carefully about the causal chain: *if this behaviour changes, why do we believe the business result will follow?*
