# Shifting from Outputs to Outcomes

The hardest part of becoming outcome-oriented isn't understanding the concept — it's making it stick. This file covers the practical and organisational challenges of the shift, and how to navigate them.

---

## Why the Shift Is Hard

When a team delivers outputs, progress is easy to see. "Did we ship the feature? Yes or no." Output-oriented teams always know how they're doing.

When a team shifts to outcomes, this visibility disappears — at least initially. "Is the team making progress toward their outcome?" is a harder question. The metric might not move for a week. The team might try three different approaches before finding one that works. This ambiguity is uncomfortable for leaders trained on output delivery.

The most common leadership question when shifting to outcomes: **"How do I know if my teams are performing?"**

This discomfort is legitimate. Acknowledging it openly — rather than pretending the shift is easy — builds trust. The answer is: you measure differently, not less. Progress on an outcome requires leading indicators, early signals, and regular check-ins on learning (not just shipping).

---

## The Trust-Autonomy Exchange

The shift to outcomes requires a genuine exchange between leadership and teams:

**Leadership gives up**: control over *what* gets built (the specific outputs, the features, the roadmap items)

**Leadership gets**: clarity on *why* work is happening (the outcome) and *whether* it's working (the metric moving)

**Teams get**: autonomy to decide *how* to move the outcome (which solutions, which experiments, which bets)

**Teams take on**: accountability for the outcome, not just the delivery

This exchange only works if both sides hold up their end. Teams that get outcome ownership but are still told exactly what to build are getting the accountability without the autonomy — the worst of both worlds. Leaders who give teams autonomy but then panic at the first ambiguous week and demand output updates haven't made the shift.

## How to Deconstruct Business Outcomes Into Product Outcomes

The practical starting point is your revenue model. Most product outcomes can be found by deconstructing business outcomes through a simple causal chain.

**Step 1: Start with the business outcome**
Pick one business metric that matters most right now. Example: reduce churn.

**Step 2: Ask "What customer behaviours drive this?"**
For a product like Legalhero: clients who successfully close a case with a positive experience are much more likely to return / refer others. So retention is driven by case resolution quality + experience.

**Step 3: Identify what the product can observe and influence**
What does "positive experience" look like *in the product*? Maybe it's: client completes all required steps without needing to contact support; client responds to lawyer requests within 24 hours; client receives a status update at every major case milestone.

**Step 4: Pick the behaviour that is most causally linked to the business outcome**
This becomes your product outcome. The others can become secondary metrics or hypotheses to test.

**The resulting chain:**
```
Business outcome: Reduce churn / increase client return rate
    ↓ driven by
Client satisfaction with case resolution
    ↓ driven by
Product outcome: % of clients who complete all required steps without support contact
    ↓ influenced by
Initiatives: [whatever the team bets will move this metric]
```

## Measuring Team Progress Without Falling Back on Output Tracking

When a team is working toward an outcome, you measure progress by asking:

1. **Is the metric moving?** Track the outcome weekly. Even small shifts are signal.
2. **Are we learning?** A team that ran 2 experiments this week and got clear results is making progress, even if the metric hasn't moved yet.
3. **Are we eliminating bad paths?** Knowing what *doesn't* move the outcome is valuable. Not every week will produce a win.
4. **What do we believe now that we didn't before?** This is the key leadership question in an outcome-oriented world. It replaces "what did you ship?"

Teams should share their **current outcome number**, **what they tried**, **what happened**, and **what they plan next**. This replaces a sprint demo of features with a weekly learning report.

## Common Sticking Points and How to Handle Them

**"We don't have the data to measure our outcome."**
Then establishing the measurement *is* the first initiative. Before you can move a metric, you need to be able to see it. Make instrumentation a priority.

**"Our outcome depends on something that takes 6 months to show up."**
Find a leading indicator — a behaviour you can observe now that predicts the lagging outcome later. Test the correlation over time and treat the leading indicator as your working target.

**"Stakeholders keep asking for specific features and don't care about outcomes."**
Translate their feature requests into the outcomes they're trying to drive. "You want us to build X — what customer behaviour are you hoping X will change? Let's make that the target, and hold X lightly as one possible way to get there."

**"We have too many outcomes."**
A team tracking five different outcomes has zero. Focus is the whole point. One strong outcome per team per quarter is ambitious. Two is usually too many. Help the team make the prioritisation call — which one outcome, if it moved, would matter most right now?

**"The outcome moved but not because of what we did."**
This is real. External factors can shift your metric (seasonality, a competitor going down, a marketing campaign). The response isn't to abandon outcome thinking — it's to instrument well enough to separate signal from noise, and to build a track record over multiple quarters.
